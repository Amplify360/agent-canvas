Scenario Framework

Scenario A — Platform Absorption (Bear Case)

CrowdStrike successfully integrates Falcon Shield + SGNL + Seraphic into a unified SaaS security offering within 12 months. Falcon Flex pricing bundles SSPM at near-zero marginal cost. Combined with Palo Alto's SSPM + CASB integration and Microsoft's MDCA distribution, standalone SSPM becomes increasingly difficult to sell as a separate line item.

Signal: Falcon Shield adoption metrics in CrowdStrike earnings; Falcon Flex SSPM attach rates; enterprises declining RFPs.

Signal: Pipeline conversion on breach-driven inbound; AI governance as a buying trigger in >15% of deals; Reco win rates stable vs. platform vendors.

EU AI Act obligations + shadow AI urgency + agentic AI proliferation create a new buying category that Reco owns. Signal: AI governance cited as primary buying trigger in >30% of new deals; EU AI Act compliance RFPs naming Reco; strategic partnership with AI/identity platform.

ShinyHunters SSO Vishing Campaign — 6+ Named Enterprise Victims
Jan–Feb 2026 ✓ Verified Company Disclosure · Threat Intelligence
▶
What Happened

ShinyHunters (a threat group that some third-party researchers associate with SCATTERED SPIDER under what has been termed the SLSH alliance) launched a targeted SSO vishing campaign that compromised at least 6 named enterprises: Panera (Jan 2026, incident confirmed, reported data exposure), CarGurus (Feb 2026, employee data accessed), Figure (Feb 2026, personal data exposure), Betterment (Jan 2026), Optimizely (Feb 2026), and a sixth confirmed but unnamed victim. Attack pattern: social engineering → SSO credential theft → lateral SaaS access across connected applications. Mandiant (via Google Cloud) confirmed the campaign is ongoing and likely has additional undisclosed victims.

Why This Matters for Reco

This is a live, real-time validation of Reco's core value proposition: SaaS identity visibility and threat detection. The attack vector — SSO credential compromise leading to cross-SaaS lateral movement — is exactly what Reco's identity graph and ITDR are designed to detect and disrupt. Every board member in every Reco-adjacent deal just saw their threat model validated.

Strategic Implication for Reco

Breach urgency creates a finite window of heightened CISO attention. The board should push for: (1) a forensic demo capability showing how Reco's graph would have detected the ShinyHunters attack pattern, (2) conversion metrics on breach-driven inbound pipeline, and (3) whether the company is seeing a different buyer profile or ACV in urgency-driven deals.

So what: If Reco cannot demonstrate — with forensic specificity — how its identity graph would have detected the ShinyHunters SSO-to-SaaS lateral movement pattern, the breach urgency benefits all SaaS security vendors equally rather than disproportionately favoring Reco.

OAuth Token Abuse Wave — SaaS-to-SaaS Lateral Movement via Compromised Integrations
Aug 2025 – Feb 2026 ✓ Verified Security Research · Incident Disclosure
▶
What Happened

Three distinct OAuth token abuse incidents surfaced over this period: (1) Salesloft/Drift OAuth tokens used to access Salesforce customer data (Aug 2025); (2) Gainsight OAuth integration exploited to pivot into Salesforce environments (Nov 2025, detected Nov 19); (3) Microsoft Copilot Studio "CoPhish" technique — Datadog Security Labs demonstrated using AI agents as OAuth consent phishing wrappers, automating credential harvesting at scale (Oct 2025). The CISA shadow AI incident (acting CISA director uploading FOUO documents to public ChatGPT, Jul 2025 — reported Jan 2026) adds a fourth vector: insider misuse of AI SaaS.

Why This Matters for Reco

OAuth token abuse represents SaaS-to-SaaS lateral movement — the category of attack that existing network security and endpoint detection fundamentally cannot see. This validates the need for a SaaS-native identity and integration monitoring layer. The CoPhish technique is particularly significant: it demonstrates that AI agents themselves become attack infrastructure, blurring the line between "authorized tool" and "threat vector."

Strategic Implication for Reco

The board should assess: (1) Can Reco demonstrate detection of OAuth token abuse patterns with specificity? (2) Is the company's AI governance positioning backed by product capability to detect CoPhish-style agent abuse? (3) Are prospects citing these incidents in evaluations? The move from theoretical AI risk to demonstrated AI-enabled attacks accelerates the urgency for Reco's AI governance capabilities.

(2) How many recent lost deals were "losses to platform renewal" rather than competitive evaluations? (3) At what point does CrowdStrike's assembled stack reach "good enough" depth? Forrester estimates at least 18–24 months for complete integration of acquisitions like these — that window is Reco's opportunity.

So what: At ~$1.37B deployed, CrowdStrike has spent more than 16× Reco's total funding assembling a competing stack. The strategic question is not whether CrowdStrike's stack will be "good enough" — it's when — and whether Reco has converted enough installed base before that happens.

Questions for the Board Discussion

Question 1 — Invisible Pipeline Loss

"What percentage of our lost deals in the last two quarters were competitive losses to a platform vendor where SSPM was added to an existing contract renewal — and how many deals did we never even enter because no standalone RFP was issued? What percentage of our target accounts have existing CrowdStrike Falcon contracts?"
Surfaces the pipeline loss that win/loss data doesn't capture. If buyers add SSPM to platform renewals without evaluating standalone vendors, Reco's TAM is shrinking invisibly. The Falcon Flex drawdown model makes this particularly acute — a CISO can add SSPM without new budget approval or an RFP.

Question 2 — Identity Governance Strategic Fork

"Saviynt's $700M raise positions identity governance as the foundation for AI-era security. Where does Reco's identity graph roadmap go — are we building toward identity governance use cases, or partnering with IGA platforms? Is our identity graph a complement (partnership opportunity) or a competitor (budget conflict)? What's the 12-month plan?"
Forces a strategic choice. If Reco's identity graph competes with IGA platforms for the same budget line, it's bringing $85M to a $700M fight. If it complements them, there's a partnership and co-sell opportunity that could accelerate distribution.

Question 3 — Breach-Driven Demand Conversion

"The ShinyHunters SSO campaign and the OAuth token abuse wave are creating acute urgency for SaaS identity security. Can we demonstrate, with specificity, how Reco's identity graph and ITDR would have detected or disrupted the attack pattern used against Panera, CarGurus, and Figure? What's the conversion rate on breach-driven inbound vs. our core outbound pipeline — and are we seeing a different buyer profile or ACV in breach-driven deals?"
Tests whether Reco is converting external urgency into pipeline and revenue — or whether the demand catalyst is benefiting all competitors equally. The specificity question matters: can Reco show a prospect exactly how the ShinyHunters attack would have been caught?

Question 4 — AI Governance Revenue Signal

"Of deals closed in the last two quarters, how many cited 'AI governance' or 'shadow AI discovery' as the primary buying trigger versus traditional SSPM — and what is the ACV difference between those buyer profiles?"
Tests whether AI governance positioning is translating into revenue or remains a marketing narrative. If AI governance deals are higher ACV and growing as a share of pipeline, the "AI Governance Breakout" scenario is materializing. If not, the positioning may need to be backed by more concrete product capabilities.

Question 5 — Integration Moat Durability

C. Possible Ideas for Enhancements (Not Exhaustive)

  • Temporal Framing Tag each item as New This Quarter, Ongoing / Escalated, or Resolved Since Last Meeting. Allows board members who have seen prior briefs to immediately identify what changed — reducing re-reading of known context and surfacing escalation patterns across meetings.
  • Cross-Portfolio Intelligence At scale across Insight Partners' 500+ portfolio companies, flag when an external event impacts multiple portfolio companies simultaneously. From this brief alone: the ShinyHunters campaign may affect other portfolio companies with SSO-dependent SaaS architectures; CrowdStrike's M&A spree impacts any portfolio company competing with or selling alongside CrowdStrike modules; DORA and EU AI Act obligations apply across the portfolio's EU-exposed companies. Cross-portfolio tagging transforms company-level intelligence into portfolio-level situational awareness.
  • Board Member Personalization Where a board member's known priorities are available (e.g., focused on M&A readiness, or de-prioritizing a product line), weight severity ranking and item selection accordingly. A board member focused on international expansion would see DORA and EU AI Act items elevated; one focused on competitive positioning would see CrowdStrike and Obsidian items prioritized.
  • Source Expansion This brief relied on publicly available sources: SEC filings, company press releases, government databases, security research publications, and credible industry media. Production enhancements could include: patent and trademark filings (early signal of competitor product direction), job postings (hiring patterns as leading indicators of GTM or product expansion), app marketplace listings (new integrations on Salesforce AppExchange, AWS Marketplace), earnings call transcripts (for public competitors such as CrowdStrike and Palo Alto Networks), and licensed analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC positioning data).
  • Source Confidence Tiering Systematically tier sources by independence: (1) Primary independent — SEC filings, government databases, court records; (2) Independent reporting — credentialed journalists with named sources; (3) Vendor-produced — company press releases, vendor-commissioned surveys and analyst reports. This brief noted vendor-produced sources where applicable (e.g., AppOmni survey, Frost & Sullivan / CrowdStrike growth figure) but a systematic tiering would further increase board member trust in the output.
